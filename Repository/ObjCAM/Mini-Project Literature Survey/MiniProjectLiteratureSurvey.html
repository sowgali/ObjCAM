Black-Box adversarial attacks on videos

Sowrya Gali - CS18BTECH11012

Introduction

Deep neural networks are known to be vulnerable to adversarial attacks. These have undergone small, carefully crafted perturbations, and can easily fool DNNs into making wrong decisions. Most of the emphasis, in adversarial attacks, has been on image data. The study on adversarial attacks for videos has started recently, and much work has been done in the area of White-Box attacks. In this survey report, we try to summarize various Black-Box attacks on video data.

Attack Strategies

Attacking Optical Flow[1]

This attack’s basic idea is to inject an adversarial patch into an image to make the optical flow prediction wrong. Anurag Ranjan proposes this attack. A successful adversarial attack would cause massive changes in estimated optical flow for small, unnoticeable, perturbations in the images. The optical networks can be broadly classified into two types: the encoder-decoder and spatial pyramid networks.

In the proposed method, the perturbation directions are initialized with the samples from other classes different from the clean video class. Meanwhile, it heuristically searches a subset of frames from the clean video with the initialized directions, and the salient regions detected from the selected frames are utilized to update the initial directions.

Metrics

  • Success Rate: the ratio of successful generation of adversarial examples under the perturbations bound within the limit of number of queries.
  • Try to find data on which it is used.
  • Fooling rate (FR): the ratio of adversarial videos that are successfully misclassified.
  • ANQ: average number of queries, required for a successful attack (excluding failed  attacks).
  • Median queries (MQ): the median number of queries.
  • Mean absolute perturbation(MAP): denotes the mean perturbation of each pixel in the entire video.
  • Sparsity (S): represents the proportion of pixels with no perturbations versus all pixels in a specific (frame??).

Comparative Conjugates

  1. (Flickering paper) contrasted FR, MAP and MAT. (Kinetics-400)
  2. (Heuristics paper) contrasted MAP, MAP*, S, MQ (UCF-101 and HMDB-51)
  3. (V-BAD paper) contrasted SR, AQ (UCF-101, HMDB-51, Kinetics-400)
  4. (Optical Flow paper) used EPE as sole metric (KITTI)

These conjugates help us to understand the different buckets into which we can classify the metrics. And usually one metric from each bucket is used to evaluate the performance of the attack.

Inference about GradCAM++ colors

The colored feature maps produced from GradCAM++ are called saliency maps/heat maps. And what they essentially depict is the activation given by pixels for the neurons of a particular class. The hotter the region, the higher the activation due the pixels from that region. The redder the region, the hotter it is. It follows the VIBGYOR order with increasing degrees of hotness.

Therefore the red regions play a crucial role in determining the class of the image compared to the other regions.

The approach is to use the YOLOv3 model itself and use the gradients of the penultimate layer to the yolo layer i.e, the fully convolutional layer with 1x1 convolutions and linear activation.

Since the input maps and output scores are linearly related to each other the derivatives are weights themselves. The final activation is sigmoid and hence we can derive the weights for the equation similar to GradCAM++, and obtain weights of the heatmaps. These weights come in three scales as is the case with YOLO so we superimpose them to form one final big heatmap.

From the above results we can infer that this model generates well to some of the examples and not so good for others. And one observation is that the “temperature” does not lie on the edges of the image, contrary to the intuition.

Derivation for ObjCAM

In the YOLOv3 model the predictions happen at the ‘yolo’ layer. And there are three ‘yolo’ layers since YOLOv3 predicts at three different scales viz., 13x13, 26x26, and 52x52. And each of these ‘yolo’ layers are preceded by a 1x1 convolutional layer(FCN) with linear activation, which acts as a convolutional counterpart for a fully-connected dense layer.

  1. Go to the GradCAM directory and put your VGG-trained weight file in the “models” sub-directory, and rename it to “vgg16.npy”.
  2. Now come to base directory, you need to change the number of class from 43 to your required in the following files:
  1. models/Vgg.py
  2. models/Vgg_utils.py
  3. misc/utils.py
  1. Run the command “python3 classify.py -f <ImageFile> -o <outname>. Outputs will be stored in the “out” directory.
